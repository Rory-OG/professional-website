## Stop Trying to Sit Up Straight

For years, my approach to posture was the same as everyone else's: notice I'm slouching, forcibly correct it, hold the correction for about ninety seconds, forget, and repeat. This cycle felt like a personal failing — a lack of discipline. It turns out it's just a misunderstanding of what posture actually is.

Posture isn't a position you hold. It's a dynamic, continuously regulated output of your nervous system. Your brain is integrating signals from your vestibular system, your visual field, proprioceptors in your joints and fascia, and the current state of your muscle tone — and it's producing a postural strategy that reflects what it considers optimal given all that input. When you "correct" your posture by brute-forcing your shoulders back, you're overriding that system temporarily without changing any of the inputs. Of course it doesn't stick. You haven't changed the system; you've just argued with its output.

## Proprioception and the Maps Your Body Uses

The insight that changed my thinking was learning about proprioceptive maps — the internal representation your nervous system maintains of where your body is in space. These maps are use-dependent. If you spend eight hours a day in a particular position, your nervous system updates its model of "normal" to reflect that position. Your proprioceptive baseline shifts. This means that when you do sit up with good alignment, it doesn't feel neutral — it feels effortful and wrong, because it doesn't match the map. You're not fighting laziness; you're fighting a well-calibrated sensory prediction.

This is where the connection to habit formation gets interesting. Charles Duhigg's habit loop — cue, routine, reward — maps almost perfectly onto postural behaviour. The cue is a sensory state (discomfort, a glance at your reflection, a moment of body awareness). The routine is the postural correction. But the reward is where it breaks down: there's no immediate reward for good posture, and the "corrected" position actually feels uncomfortable because it conflicts with your proprioceptive map. You've set up a habit loop with a negative reward signal. No wonder it doesn't consolidate.

## Changing the System, Not the Output

What actually works — at least in my experience and based on the motor learning literature — is changing the inputs to the system rather than fighting the output. That means variability of movement throughout the day rather than one "correct" position. It means exercises that update proprioceptive maps: slow, controlled movements through full ranges of motion where you're paying attention to the sensory experience, not just cranking through reps. It means addressing the nervous system's threat assessment, because postural guarding patterns are often driven by a nervous system that's in a protective state — and no amount of stretching resolves a nervous system issue.

## The Broader Lesson

The broader lesson I take from this is about habits in general. When a behaviour isn't sticking, the instinct is to blame willpower or motivation. But often the problem is that you're trying to override a system without understanding what the system is optimising for. Your nervous system isn't stupid — it's producing the posture that makes sense given the information it has. If you want different output, give it different information. The same principle applies to productivity habits, sleep hygiene, exercise consistency — almost anything where you're trying to change a default behaviour. Understand the system first. Then change the inputs. The output will follow.
