## The Honest Premise

I want to be upfront about something: most ADHD productivity advice is written by people who figured out a system that worked for three months and are writing about it during the honeymoon phase. I've been that person. I've enthusiastically recommended apps, methods, and routines that I'd completely abandoned by the time the post got any traction. So this is less "here's the answer" and more "here's what's survived long enough to be worth mentioning, along with some honest notes about failure modes."

The core principle that everything else hangs off is this: my working memory is unreliable, my time perception is essentially fictional, and my ability to initiate tasks is inconsistent. These aren't character flaws — they're the operating parameters of my particular brain. The goal isn't to fix them; it's to build external scaffolding that compensates for them. Every tool and strategy I use is evaluated against one question: does this reduce the cognitive load required to do the thing, or does it add a new thing I have to remember to do? If it's the latter, it will fail within a month.

## External Scaffolding That Survived

The things that have lasted are almost embarrassingly simple. A single physical notebook that lives on my desk — not a fancy system, just a running list of what I'm doing today, rewritten each morning. The rewriting is the point; it forces a daily re-engagement with priorities rather than letting yesterday's list carry forward on autopilot. A single digital task manager with exactly two views: "today" and "everything else." Anything more granular than that creates an organisational task that becomes its own procrastination vector. Time-blocking in 45-minute chunks with forced transitions — not because 45 minutes is optimal, but because without an external signal to stop, I'll either abandon a task at minute twelve or look up from hyperfocus at hour four having missed two meetings.

## Environmental Design and Transition Costs

The biggest lever, honestly, is environmental design. ADHD brains are disproportionately affected by transition costs — the cognitive overhead of switching from one task to another. So I've gotten ruthless about reducing transitions. My workspace has dedicated zones: one screen configuration for deep work, one for communication, and switching between them is a single keystroke macro that changes the entire context. I keep my phone in a different room during focus blocks, not because I lack self-control, but because the presence of the option to check it is itself a cognitive tax, even if I never pick it up. This isn't willpower — it's architecture.

## Leveraging Hyperfocus (Carefully)

Hyperfocus is the ADHD superpower that every LinkedIn post celebrates and that almost nobody talks about honestly. Yes, I can lock in for six hours on a problem and produce work that would take most people two days. But hyperfocus isn't controllable or predictable. I can't summon it for the task that's actually due. It arrives when it arrives, usually for the task that's most intellectually stimulating, which is rarely the task that's most important. So my approach now is to ride it when it shows up, but never to depend on it. I plan my weeks assuming zero hyperfocus sessions, so that when they happen, they're a bonus rather than a requirement.

## What Doesn't Work (For Me)

I want to close with honesty about what doesn't work, because I think the ADHD productivity space has a survivorship bias problem. Habit tracking apps don't work for me — maintaining the tracking habit is harder than maintaining the actual habit. Pomodoro technique doesn't work — the 25-minute blocks are too short to get into flow and the breaks derail me. "Eating the frog" (doing the hardest task first) doesn't work — my brain isn't online enough in the morning for hard tasks, and forcing it just creates an aversive association with the start of the day. Your mileage will vary on all of this. The only universal advice I'd give is: pay attention to what actually works for you over a timescale of months, not days, and be willing to discard anything that stops working, no matter how much you've invested in the system.
