## The Counting Trap

Most People Analytics teams I've worked with can tell you their attrition rate to two decimal places. They can segment it by department, tenure band, performance rating, and manager. They've built dashboards that update in near real-time and they can show you year-over-year trends with confidence intervals. And almost none of that tells you anything useful about why people are leaving.

This isn't a criticism of the teams themselves — it's a structural problem. When PA functions are stood up, they're usually given a mandate to "bring data to HR decisions." So they start with what's measurable: headcount, turnover, time-to-fill, cost-per-hire. These are lagging indicators. By the time they move, the interesting part is already over. You're doing an autopsy when what you needed was a diagnostic.

## Lagging Indicators vs. Understanding Systems

The distinction I keep coming back to is the difference between measurement and understanding. Measurement asks "what happened?" Understanding asks "what's happening, and what conditions make it likely to keep happening?" These are fundamentally different questions, and they require fundamentally different approaches.

A team that measures attrition will notice a spike in Q3. A team that understands the system will have noticed the shift in internal mobility patterns, the compression in pay bands relative to market, and the change in skip-level meeting frequency that preceded the spike by six months. The second team isn't smarter — they're just looking at the right layer of the system.

## What Leading Indicators Actually Look Like

When I talk about leading indicators in people analytics, I'm not talking about engagement survey scores. Engagement surveys, as typically deployed, are themselves lagging indicators dressed up in proactive clothing. By the time someone tells you they're disengaged on a five-point scale, the disengagement has been baked in for months.

Real leading indicators tend to be behavioural and relational. Changes in collaboration network density. Shifts in the ratio of internal to external job applications. The velocity of information flow between teams. These are harder to measure, harder to explain to stakeholders, and harder to build dashboards around. That's precisely why most teams don't bother with them — and why the teams that do tend to produce genuinely useful insights.

## Moving Toward Systems Thinking

The shift I'd advocate for is from a reporting function to a systems-sensing function. This doesn't mean abandoning your headcount reports — those still matter for operational governance. But it means recognising that the real value of a PA team isn't in telling leaders what already happened. It's in helping them understand the dynamics that will shape what happens next. That requires different skills (network analysis, causal inference, qualitative synthesis), different data sources (collaboration metadata, calendar patterns, organisational network analysis), and frankly, a different relationship with the business. You have to be close enough to the work to know which signals matter, and independent enough to say things that are uncomfortable.
