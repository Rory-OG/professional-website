## The Technical Readiness Fallacy

There's a pattern I keep seeing in AI transformation projects. An organisation runs a technical readiness assessment — do we have the infrastructure, the data pipelines, the security protocols? They score well, they roll out the tooling, and then adoption flatlines at 15%. Leadership is baffled. The technology works. The training was delivered. The champions were identified. And yet most people quietly went back to their spreadsheets within three weeks.

The problem is that technical readiness and psychological readiness are different constructs, and almost nobody is assessing the second one. IO Psychology has been studying technology adoption in workplaces for decades, and the frameworks that exist — the Technology Acceptance Model, the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology, self-determination theory applied to workplace tools — predict adoption behaviour far better than any infrastructure audit.

## What TAM Actually Tells Us

The Technology Acceptance Model is straightforward: people adopt technology when they perceive it as useful and when they perceive it as easy to use. That sounds obvious, but the implications are subtle. Perceived usefulness isn't the same as actual usefulness. A tool can genuinely save someone two hours a week, but if they don't perceive that saving — if the cognitive overhead of switching makes the old way feel faster — adoption won't happen. This is where most AI rollouts go wrong. They demonstrate capability ("look what this can do") instead of demonstrating fit ("here's how this slots into the workflow you already have").

Perceived ease of use is equally misunderstood. It's not about whether the tool is objectively simple. It's about whether the user believes they can operate it competently without looking foolish. In organisations where AI literacy is low and anxiety is high, even a well-designed interface can feel threatening. The IO Psychology literature on self-efficacy — Bandura's work and its extensions — tells us exactly how to address this: mastery experiences, social modelling, and reducing physiological anxiety cues. Most training programmes do none of these things. They do webinars.

## Change Management Needs Psychology, Not Just Process

The other piece that gets missed is that AI adoption is fundamentally a change management challenge, and effective change management is grounded in psychology. Kotter's model, Bridges' transition model, even the ADKAR framework — these all describe psychological transitions, not project management milestones. The "resistance" that leaders complain about isn't irrational. It's a predictable response to perceived loss of autonomy, competence threat, and uncertainty about role identity. These are well-studied constructs in IO Psychology, and there are evidence-based interventions for each of them.

## Bridging the Gap

What I'd like to see — and what I try to do in the projects I'm involved with — is AI adoption strategies that are co-designed with IO psychologists or at least informed by the literature. That means pre-deployment assessment of psychological readiness: perceived usefulness expectations, self-efficacy beliefs, trust in the technology, and clarity about how roles will evolve. It means designing adoption pathways that create early mastery experiences rather than overwhelming users with full feature sets. And it means treating resistance as data rather than as an obstacle. When someone pushes back on using an AI tool, the interesting question isn't "how do we get them to comply?" It's "what is their resistance telling us about the gap between our deployment strategy and their lived experience of work?"
